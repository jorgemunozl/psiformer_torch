\begin{abstract}
The many-electron Schr\"odinger equation is a central problem in \emph{ab initio} quantum chemistry. Variational Monte Carlo (VMC) with flexible neural ans\"atze, as in FermiNet and related architectures, has shown that deep models can approximate ground-state wavefunctions accurately. This work presents a PyTorch-based study of a Slater--Jastrow ansatz in which a transformer-style backbone parameterizes single-particle orbitals entering a Slater determinant, together with a low-parameter Jastrow factor enforcing electron--electron cusp structure. We summarize the governing equations, the VMC objective and Metropolis--Hastings sampling, implementation choices for stable derivatives of the log-determinant via SVD, and batched energy evaluation for high GPU utilization. Representative convergence curves and energy estimates illustrate the behavior of the resulting \emph{Psiformer}-style model on small molecular benchmarks.
\end{abstract}

\begin{keywords}
Many-electron Schr\"odinger equation, variational Monte Carlo, transformer, neural wavefunction, Slater determinant, quantum chemistry.
\end{keywords}

\section{Introduction}

Computing the ground state of many-electron systems requires solving the electronic Schr\"odinger equation in the presence of Coulomb interactions and fermionic antisymmetry. Deep variational wavefunctions trained with VMC have emerged as a practical route to high-accuracy energies without an explicit basis expansion in the traditional sense \cite{ferminet,psiformer}. Self-attention mechanisms, as introduced for sequence modeling \cite{attention}, motivate architectures that mix information across electrons while respecting permutation structure when combined with a Slater determinant. This article collects the material from an expository slide deck into article form and documents implementation aspects of a Psiformer-style ansatz in PyTorch.

\section{The Schr\"odinger wave function and physical constraints}

\subsection{Schr\"odinger equation}

In 1926 Schr\"odinger formulated the time-dependent Schr\"odinger equation (TDSE),
\begin{equation}
	i\,\hbar\,\partial_t \Psi = \hat{H}\,\Psi.
\end{equation}
Here $\Psi \in \mathcal{H}$ is the \textbf{wave function}, $\hat{H}$ is the \textbf{Hamiltonian}, and the state depends on spatial coordinates and time. The quantity $|\Psi|^2$ is interpreted as a probability density (normalized to integrate to one).

In the position representation,
\begin{equation}
	\hat{H}=\frac{\hat{\vec{P}}^{2}}{2m}+\hat{V}
	=-\frac{\hbar^{2}}{2m}\nabla^{2}+\hat{V}.
\end{equation}

Separating $\psi(\vec{\mathbf{r}},t)=R(\vec{\mathbf{r}})T(t)$ leads to
\begin{equation}
	T(t)=e^{-iEt/\hbar}, \qquad \hat{H}R(\vec{\mathbf{r}})=ER(\vec{\mathbf{r}}),
\end{equation}
with $E$ the total energy. The stationary problem is to find $\psi$ such that
\begin{equation}
	\left[-\frac{\hbar^{2}}{2m}\nabla^{2}+V(\mathbf{r},t)\right]\psi(\vec{\mathbf{r}})= E \psi(\vec{\mathbf{r}}),
\end{equation}
given the potential $V$ of the system.

\subsection{Many-body Hamiltonian}

For $n$ electrons (and clamped nuclei in the Born--Oppenheimer approximation), collective coordinates $\mathbf{x}_i=(\mathbf{r}_i,\sigma_i)$ combine position $\mathbf{r}_i\in\mathbb{R}^3$ and spin $\sigma_i\in\{\uparrow,\downarrow\}$. The eigenproblem reads
\begin{equation}
	\hat{H}\psi(\mathbf{x}_{1},\dots ,\mathbf{x}_{n})=E\psi(\mathbf{x}_{1},\dots ,\mathbf{x}_{n}).
\end{equation}
Each particle interacts with all others; for atoms one would include nuclei and electrons consistently, subject to the chosen level of approximation.

A standard molecular Hamiltonian (atomic units) takes the form
\begin{align}
	\hat H & =
	-\sum_{i=1}^{N}\frac{1}{2}\nabla_i^{2}
	-\sum_{I=1}^{M}\frac{1}{2M_I}\nabla_{I}^{2}
	-\sum_{i=1}^{N}\sum_{I=1}^{M}\frac{Z_I}{|\mathbf r_i-\mathbf R_I|} \notag\\
	&\quad +\sum_{1\le i<j\le N}\frac{1}{|\mathbf r_i-\mathbf r_j|}
	+\sum_{1\le I<J\le M}\frac{Z_I Z_J}{|\mathbf R_I-\mathbf R_J|}.
\end{align}
The Born--Oppenheimer approximation fixes nuclear positions and often drops the nuclear kinetic terms when solving for the electronic wavefunction.

\subsection{Fermi--Dirac statistics and cusps}

Electrons are indistinguishable fermions: exchanging two electrons flips the sign of $\Psi$, and the Pauli principle forbids double occupation of the same spin orbital. A Slater determinant built from spin orbitals $\phi_j^k(\mathbf{x}_i)$ enforces antisymmetry,
\begin{equation}
	\psi=
	\begin{vmatrix}
		\phi_{1}^{k}(\mathbf{x}_{1})  & \dots  &  \phi_{1}^{k}(\mathbf{x}_{n}) \\
		\vdots   &  \ddots  & \vdots  \\
		\phi_{n}^{k}(\mathbf{x}_{1}) & \dots & \phi_{n}^{k}(\mathbf{x}_{n})
	\end{vmatrix}.
\end{equation}

Coulomb interactions induce cusps in $\Psi$ when particles coalesce. Kato-type cusp conditions include electron--nucleus
\begin{equation*}
	\frac{\partial \Psi}{\partial r_{iI}}\Big|_{r_{iI}=0} = -\,Z_I\,\Psi(0)
\end{equation*}
and electron--electron
\begin{equation*}
	\frac{\partial \Psi}{\partial r_{ij}}\Big|_{r_{ij}=0} = \frac{1}{2}\,\Psi(0).
\end{equation*}
A Jastrow factor $\exp(\mathcal{J})$ is often used to impose short-range correlation structure. In this work we use
\begin{equation}
	\mathcal{J}_{\theta}(\mathbf{x})
	=\sum_{\substack{i<j\\ \sigma_{i}=\sigma_{j}}}
	-\frac{1}{4}\frac{\alpha^{2}_{\mathrm{par}}}{\alpha_{\mathrm{par}}+|\mathbf{r}_{i}-\mathbf{r}_{j}|}
	+\sum_{\substack{i,j\\ \sigma_{i}\neq \sigma_{j}}}
	-\frac{1}{2}\frac{\alpha^{2}_{\mathrm{anti}}}{\alpha_{\mathrm{anti}}+|\mathbf{r}_{i}-\mathbf{r}_{j}|}.
\end{equation}

\section{Variational Monte Carlo}

\subsection{Variational principle and local energy}

The variational principle states
\begin{equation}
	E[\Psi] = \frac{\bra{\Psi} \hat{H}\ket{\Psi}}{\braket{\Psi}{\Psi}} \ge E_0.
\end{equation}
Minimizing $E[\Psi]$ with respect to parameters drives the ansatz toward the ground state. Writing $\Psi_\theta$ for a parameterized trial function,
\begin{equation}
	\mathcal{L}(\Psi_\theta)
	=\frac{\bra{\Psi_{\theta}} \hat{H}\ket{\Psi_{\theta}}}{\braket{\Psi_{\theta}}{\Psi_{\theta}}}
	=\frac{\int \Psi_\theta^*(\mathbf{R})\,\hat{H}\Psi_\theta(\mathbf{R})\,d\mathbf{R}}{\int \Psi_\theta^*(\mathbf{R})\Psi_\theta(\mathbf{R})\,d\mathbf{R}}.
\end{equation}
With the normalized density $p_\theta(\mathbf{R})\propto|\Psi_\theta(\mathbf{R})|^2$ and the \emph{local energy}
\begin{equation}
	E_L(\mathbf{R})=\frac{\hat{H}\Psi_\theta(\mathbf{R})}{\Psi_\theta(\mathbf{R})},
\end{equation}
the loss is the expectation $\mathcal{L}_\theta=\mathbb{E}_{\mathbf{R}\sim p_\theta}[E_L(\mathbf{R})]$.

\subsection{Monte Carlo estimation and sampling}

Given samples $\mathbf{R}_1,\dots,\mathbf{R}_M$ drawn from $|\Psi_\theta|^2$ (up to normalization),
\begin{equation}
	\mathcal{L}_\theta
	=\mathbb{E}_{\mathbf{R}\sim |\Psi_\theta|^2}[E_L(\mathbf{R})]
	\approx \frac{1}{M}\sum_{k=1}^{M} E_L(\mathbf{R}_k).
\end{equation}
The local energy can be written as
\begin{equation}
	E_L(\mathbf{R}_k)
	=\frac{\hat{H}\psi(\mathbf{R}_k)}{\psi(\mathbf{R}_k)}
	=-\frac{1}{2}\frac{\nabla^{2}\psi(\mathbf{R}_k)}{\psi(\mathbf{R}_k)}+V(\mathbf{R}_k).
\end{equation}
Samples are generated with Metropolis--Hastings: starting from $\mathbf{R}_0$, propose $\mathbf{R}'=\mathbf{R}_0+\eta$ with $\eta\sim q$, accept with probability
\begin{equation}
	A(\mathbf{R}_0,\mathbf{R}')=\min\left(1,\frac{\rho(\mathbf{R}')}{\rho(\mathbf{R}_0)}\right),
\end{equation}
where $\rho\propto|\Psi_\theta|^2$, and iterate. Gradients of the VMC objective follow as (schematically)
\begin{equation}
	\nabla_{\theta}\mathcal{L}
	=2\,\mathbb{E}_{\mathbf{x}\sim |\Psi|^2}\bigl[(E_L(\mathbf{x})-\mathbb{E}_p[E_L])\,\nabla_\theta\log\Psi_\theta(\mathbf{x})\bigr],
\end{equation}
estimated with the same samples.

\section{Transformer backbone and self-attention}

Figure~\ref{fig:transformer} sketches a transformer-style backbone. Learnable matrices $W^Q,W^K,W^V\in\mathbb{R}^{n_{\text{embd}}\times n_{\text{embd}}}$ map hidden states $\mathbf{h}_i$ to queries, keys, and values,
\begin{align}
	\mathbf{q}_i &= W^Q \mathbf{h}_i, &
	\mathbf{k}_i &= W^K \mathbf{h}_i, &
	\mathbf{v}_i &= W^V \mathbf{h}_i.
\end{align}
Heads split embeddings so that $\mathbf{q}=[\mathbf{q}_1;\dots;\mathbf{q}_{n_h}]$ (and similarly $\mathbf{k},\mathbf{v}$) with per-head dimension $d_h$. In head $i$,
\begin{equation}
	\mathbf{o}_{t,i}
	=\sum_{j=1}^{t}
	\mathrm{Softmax}\left(\frac{\mathbf{q}_{t,i}^{\mathsf T}\mathbf{k}_{j,i}}{\sqrt{d_h}}\right)\mathbf{v}_{j,i},
\end{equation}
and $\mathbf{u}_t=W^O[\mathbf{o}_{t,1};\dots;\mathbf{o}_{t,n_h}]$ for an output projection $W^O$.

\section{Psiformer ansatz and architecture}

The trial wavefunction is an \emph{ansatz}: a model chosen by design and optimized by VMC. We require antisymmetry under particle exchange and expressiveness for strong electron--correlation effects. Following \cite{psiformer,ferminet}, we use a Slater--Jastrow form,
\begin{equation}
	\Psi_{\theta}(\mathbf{R})
	=\underbrace{\exp\bigl(\mathcal{J}_{\theta}(\mathbf{R})\bigr)}_{\text{Coulomb correlations}}
	\;\times\;
	\underbrace{\sum_k \omega_k \det\bigl[\boldsymbol{\phi}_{\theta}^{k}(\mathbf{R})\bigr]}_{\text{antisymmetry}}.
\end{equation}

\subsection{Laplacian via automatic differentiation}

The kinetic term requires $\nabla^2\Psi(\mathbf{R})=\sum_i \partial^2\Psi/\partial R_i^2$. In PyTorch, repeated \texttt{autograd.grad} calls accumulate second derivatives:
\begin{lstlisting}[language=Python]
R = R_o.requires_grad_(True)     # particle coordinates
psi = model(R)                   # neural wavefunction
grad_psi = torch.autograd.grad(
    psi, R,
    create_graph=True,
)[0]
laplacian = 0.0
for i in range(R.shape[-1]):
    laplacian += torch.autograd.grad(
        grad_psi[..., i], R,
        retain_graph=True
    )[0][..., i]
\end{lstlisting}

\subsection{Stable $\log\det$ gradient}

Since $\Psi_\theta\propto\det\boldsymbol{\Phi}_\theta(\mathbf{R})$, training uses $\log|\det\boldsymbol{\Phi}|$ for stability. The matrix derivative identity $\partial(\det A)/\partial A=\det(A)A^{-\mathsf T}$ becomes ill-conditioned near singular $\boldsymbol{\Phi}$. An SVD-based custom autograd op implements $\nabla_{\!A}\log|\det A|$ without explicitly forming unstable inverses:
\begin{lstlisting}[language=Python,basicstyle=\ttfamily\scriptsize]
import torch

class StableLogDet(torch.autograd.Function):
    @staticmethod
    def forward(ctx, A):
        U, S, Vh = torch.linalg.svd(A)
        ctx.save_for_backward(U, S, Vh)
        return S.log().sum()

    @staticmethod
    def backward(ctx, g):
        U, S, Vh = ctx.saved_tensors
        inv_S = torch.diag_embed(1.0 / S)
        grad_A = U @ inv_S @ Vh
        return g * grad_A
\end{lstlisting}

\subsection{Optimizer and batching}

AdamW decouples weight decay from the adaptive gradient scaling of Adam. With $\lambda$ a weight-decay coefficient and $\eta$ the learning rate,
\begin{align*}
	g_t &= \nabla \mathcal{L} + \textcolor{red}{\lambda \theta_t}, &
	\theta_{t+1} &= \theta_t - \mathrm{AdamStep}(g_t) && \text{(Adam)},\\
	g_t &= \nabla \mathcal{L}, &
	\theta_{t+1} &= \theta_t - \mathrm{AdamStep}(g_t) - \textcolor{green!50!black}{\eta\lambda\theta_t} && \text{(AdamW)}.
\end{align*}
Naive step-by-step energy evaluation over MCMC samples yielded low GPU occupancy ($\approx 30\%$). Reshaping batches as
\begin{equation*}
	(\text{mc\_steps}, B, n_e, 3)\;\longrightarrow\;(\text{mc\_steps}\times B, n_e, 3)
\end{equation*}
for batched forward passes improved utilization to roughly $99\%$ in our runs.

\section{Discussion and future work}

Several directions remain open: pretraining with external data, reducing the cost of Laplacian computation (a training bottleneck), KFAC or other natural-gradient-style optimizers, FlashAttention-style kernels for long-range mixing, transfer across chemical systems, and empirical scaling laws for model size and data.
